What’s the point of research at a time like this?

What psychology and social work research at Teesside University can do about the big challenges of our time

Author

Christopher Wilson

The Human Element

  • Contemporary challenges like misinformation, social isolation, and trauma are often viewed through a political or economic lens.
  • At their core, however, they are fundamentally human problems, rooted in cognition, emotion, and social identity.
  • Research provides us with the tools to understand these challenges, to support individuals and communities, and to start looking for solutions.

Navigating the information maze

How our emotions are affecting what we believe and the decision we make.

Perhaps one of the biggest challenges of our time is the way that misinformation spreads online, shaping beliefs and behaviours in ways that can be deeply harmful. This is not just a technological problem, but a profoundly psychological one. Understanding how people process information, how emotions shape our judgments, and how social identity influences our sense of truth is essential for developing effective strategies to counter misinformation and promote healthier public discourse.

The Paradox of Fear


Dr. Lee Copping

For individuals with strong conspiratorial beliefs, fear-based health appeals can be counterproductive, triggering defiance instead of compliance.

“Instead, the more anxious they become about infection, the more they blame the system and react against it…”

Dr. Copping’s research reveals a critical paradox in public health messaging. It demonstrates the psychological principle of reactance, where a perceived attempt at control triggers defiance. For individuals who already distrust institutions, fear appeals are not seen as warnings but as coercion, leading to an active rejection of recommended behaviours. This work shows that public health messaging must be psychologically informed to be effective across different segments of the population.

Copping, L. (2022). Anxiety and covid-19 compliance behaviors in the UK: The moderating role of conspiratorial thinking. Personality and Individual Differences, 192, 111604.

Emotion shortcuts our judgement


Dr. Chris Wilson

Cybersecurity decisions are often driven by the “affect heuristic”—our immediate “gut feelings”—rather than logic. A fraudulent email promising a prize elicits positive feelings that can override a rational assessment of its legitimacy.

Dr. Wilson’s work provides robust evidence for the role of the affect heuristic in cybersecurity. This mental shortcut means judgments are heavily influenced by immediate emotional responses. If something feels good, we judge its risks as low; if it feels bad, we do the opposite. This insight is critical for understanding why people fall for online manipulation. Enhancing digital literacy requires educating individuals about their own cognitive and emotional biases, encouraging a pause for analytical thought when emotional stakes are high.

Van Schaik, P., Renaud, K., Wilson, C., Jansen, J., & Onibokun, J. (2020). Risk as affect: The affect heuristic in cybersecurity. Computers & Security, 90, 101651.

Co-Constructing Health Knowledge


Dr. Lauren Walker

Contrary to stereotypes, healthcare professionals are “overwhelmingly positive” toward internet-informed patients, shifting their communication strategy “from compliance to co-construction” to build a shared understanding.

Dr. Walker’s research reveals a collaborative reality in clinical practice. The goal is no longer for the patient to simply comply, but for both parties to co-construct a shared plan. This shows that the potential harms of online misinformation can be mitigated through a strong, trusting therapeutic relationship. When patients feel safe to disclose their online research, it allows clinicians to address misconceptions, validate correct information, and integrate the patient’s priorities into a shared decision-making process.

Walker, L., & Sillence, E. (2024). Examining Healthcare Professionals’ Communication Around Decision-Making with Internet-Informed Patients. Health communication, 39(6), 1094–1101.

Section 2: The Fraying of Social Bonds

Understanding and Mending Isolation.

The Human-Animal Bond


Dr. Heather Clements

Dr. Clements’ research reveals a complex reality about pets and loneliness during the pandemic. Quantitative data showed no direct association; in fact, more engagement with pets was linked to poorer outcomes, likely because those already lonely sought more comfort from them.

This is a prime example of why careful psychological research is so valuable. A superficial interpretation might be that interacting with pets is detrimental. However, a more sophisticated analysis suggests the opposite causal relationship. It is likely that individuals who were already experiencing greater loneliness were the ones who sought out more engagement from their pets as a coping mechanism. The animal is not the cause of the poor outcome but a response to it.

Clements, H., et al. (2021). Companion Animal Type and Level of Engagement Matter: A Mixed-Methods Study… Animals, 11(8), 2349.

Therapeutic Responses to Marginalisation


Dr. Alex Kyriakopoulos

For destitute asylum seekers, loneliness is an active product of social exclusion. The research highlights that a therapist’s validating presence can be a lifeline, directly counteracting the dehumanising effects of their circumstances.

This work powerfully illustrates that loneliness is not merely a passive state. For marginalised groups, it is often an active product of social and political forces. The psychological response, therefore, must be equally active. It is not about teaching “social skills” but about the fundamental human act of creating a safe, trusting, and affirming relationship, providing an antidote to the isolation engendered by systemic hostility.

Gerayeli, E., & Kyriakopoulos, A. (2024). How do therapists in the UK navigate the issue of loneliness when working with destitute asylum seekers?… Counselling and Psychotherapy Research.

The Power of Group Identity: Belonging


Dr. Paul Sander

A student’s belief that “I can do this” is powerfully reinforced by the feeling that “I belong here, with these people.” Social identification is a key predictor of wellbeing and academic confidence.

Dr. Sander’s research on “Academic Behavioural Confidence” shows that a student’s confidence is not formed in a vacuum but is powerfully reinforced by social identification and a feeling of belonging. This work shows that fostering community is a powerful, evidence-based strategy for promoting individual success.

Sander, P. (2023). TUSI. Teesside University.

The Power of Group Identity: Application


Dr. Robert Portman

The practical application of the social identity approach can be used to facilitate health-enhancing behaviour change by actively building supportive and cohesive communities.

Dr. Portman’s work on the social identity approach provides a framework for actively building supportive communities to facilitate health-enhancing behaviours. His research demonstrates that when people feel part of a community pursuing shared goals, their confidence and success rates improve.

Portman, R. (2021). An exploration and intervention development…

Building Connection from the Ground Up


Dr. Catherine O’Neill

Practical, place-based initiatives like the ‘Redcar Community Kitchen’ create a shared purpose where individuals from different backgrounds can build the informal social networks that are the bedrock of a resilient community.

Dr. O’Neill’s work in community psychology demonstrates a commitment to Asset-Based Community Development. This approach moves away from focusing on a community’s needs and instead builds upon its existing strengths. The ‘Redcar Community Kitchen’ project was designed to “bring together different parts of the community to increase social connectedness.” This shows how psychology can be applied to actively design environments that combat loneliness from the ground up.

O’Neill, C. (2022). Redcar Community Kitchen. Teesside University.

Section 3: Deconstructing Division

The Psychological Roots of Prejudice and Inequality.

The Architecture of “Reasonable” Hate


Dr. Shani Burke

A key finding from discursive psychology is that strategies used to disguise talk as ‘not racist’ are also used to justify explicitly racist talk. Understanding these linguistic tactics is the first step toward deconstructing them.

Dr. Burke’s research moves beyond simply labelling discourse as hateful and instead performs a fine-grained analysis of the rhetorical strategies used to make such views appear legitimate. By analysing tactics like appealing to “common sense” or constructing minorities as a threat, her research provides a “playbook” for how prejudice is laundered into seemingly acceptable public discourse. This work is vital for fostering a more critical and resilient civic dialogue.

Burke, S., & Demasi, M. (2021). Extreme prejudice as reasoned discourse in face-to-face Interactions. In Political Communication: Discursive Perspectives.

Co-Production and Health Equity


Professor Judith Eberhardt

“Through co-producing this intervention with Black communities and delivering it in trusted spaces, we aim to break down barriers to screening and facilitate equitable access to early diagnosis and treatment”.

Prof. Eberhardt’s research exemplifies a critical evolution in tackling systemic inequality. The concept of “co-production” rejects a model where outside “experts” design interventions for a community. Instead, it involves working with community members as equal partners. This process builds trust and ensures the final product is culturally resonant, providing a powerful, evidence-based blueprint for tackling health inequalities.

Christie-de Jong, F., & Eberhardt, J. (2025). EQUITy in Black Adult health (EQUITA). National Institute for Health and Care Research.

Stigma and Reintegration


Dr. Laura Gair

Public stigma against former offenders, “fueled by media portrayals and misconceptions,” can create a self-defeating feedback loop. It creates barriers to the very factors known to reduce reoffending, like stable housing and employment.

Dr. Gair’s research highlights a dangerous societal dynamic. Public stigma creates political pressure for policies that prioritise punishment over evidence-based rehabilitation. These policies, in turn, make successful reintegration nearly impossible, potentially undermining the goal of enhancing long-term public safety. Her work demonstrates the vital role of forensic psychology in bringing evidence to a highly emotional public debate.

Tuschick, E., et al. (2025). A systematic review of public views on the reintegration of men convicted of a sexual offense into the community. Trauma, Violence, & Abuse.

Inequality Within Systems: Recruitment


Dr. Sarah Craven-Staines

Inequality can be embedded within the very systems designed to provide care. This research focuses on creating anti-racist recruitment processes in Clinical Psychology to tackle disparity at its source.

Dr. Craven-Staines’ work is a powerful example of how psychology can apply its principles to its own gatekeeping functions. By systematically examining and aiming to reform the processes by which individuals enter the profession, this research works to ensure that the future of the profession more accurately reflects the diversity of the society it serves.

Craven-Staines, S., et al. (2023). Clinical Psychology Forum, 371, 45-50.

Inequality Within Systems: Labels


Dr. Angela Prout

The language of psychology itself can create barriers. Research on “Diagnostic labels and inequalities in care” highlights how a label can carry stigma and alter how an individual is perceived and treated by care systems.

Dr. Angela Prout’s research highlights a subtle but profound mechanism of inequality. A diagnostic label, intended to be a neutral descriptor to guide treatment, can also function as a social label. Her work demonstrates the need for constant reflection on the power and potential for bias within clinical practice.

Prout, A. (2020). Psychologists practising as Approved Clinicians…

Section 4: From Trauma to Resilience

Pathways to Healing and Growth.

Trauma in Higher Education


Dr. Nikki Carthy

Dr. Carthy’s work identified a “flourishing profile” of students who, despite high levels of trauma exposure, had a positive academic experience. This provides a roadmap for universities to proactively foster protective factors like social support and coping skills.

This research represents a crucial move away from a deterministic model where trauma inevitably leads to negative outcomes. By demonstrating that a subset of students can flourish despite adversity, and by identifying the specific psychological resources that enable this, Dr. Carthy’s work provides an evidence-based roadmap for intervention. The powerful implication is that institutions can play an active role in creating an environment where students who have experienced trauma are given the tools to thrive.

Allen, S., et al. (2024). A latent profile analysis of psychosocial factors and trauma exposure in U.K. students… Psychological Trauma: Theory, Research, Practice, and Policy.

Narratives of Post-Traumatic Growth


Dr. Jayne Finch

The “Stories of Hope” project uses digital storytelling with men who have experienced post-traumatic growth after a suicide attempt. This research focuses not on risk factors but on the life that comes after, exploring positive psychological changes.

This project exemplifies a vital shift in focus within trauma studies—from pathology to resilience and meaning-making. The methodology of digital storytelling is significant, positioning the men as active creators and experts in their own lived experience. By amplifying stories of hope, this research challenges narratives of despair and provides tangible examples of how it is possible to find new meaning and purpose in the wake of profound suffering.

O’Neill, C., et al. (2025). Stories of Hope: Using Digital Storytelling with men who have experienced post-traumatic growth following a suicide attempt.

Collective Trauma


Dr. Yasmine Dunn

Dr. Dunn’s work expands the focus from individual trauma to the crucial concept of collective trauma. When an entire community endures persecution, the trauma is a shared wound impacting social structures and cultural identity.

This perspective has profound implications for therapeutic intervention. It suggests that treating individuals in isolation is insufficient. An effective response must also address the community-level dimensions of the trauma, supporting processes of collective healing, reinforcing cultural identity, and acknowledging the ongoing social stressors of displacement and marginalisation. This demands a more socially and culturally attuned approach to healing.

Dunn, Y. (2021). Academic Biography. Teesside University.

The Long Legacy of Trauma


Dr. Kathy Burrows

Trauma has a long legacy. This research focuses on the need for specialised, informed mental health services for adult survivors of childhood sexual abuse, addressing wounds carried across the lifespan.

Dr. Kathy Burrows’ research highlights the long-term consequences of early life adversity and the critical need for informed mental health services that can address these complex issues. By focusing on the professional perspective, this research also illuminates the systemic requirements for effectively supporting survivors.

Burrows, K. (2001). Clinical Psychology Forum, 2001(2), 25-29.

The Complexity of Trauma


Dr. Alan Robert Bowman

The deepest wounds of trauma are not just about re-experiencing the past, but about how it shatters a person’s sense of self, creating “Identity loss and existential fear.”

Dr. Alan Robert Bowman’s clinical work broadens our understanding of trauma’s reach. His focus recognizes that healing pushes the psychological response beyond symptom management to the more profound work of helping individuals reconstruct meaning, purpose, and a coherent sense of identity in the aftermath of life-altering experiences.

Bowman, A. (n.d.). Dr Alan Bowman, Clinical Psychologist. Psychology Today.

Section 5: Fostering Wellbeing Across the Lifespan

From Childhood to Chronic Illness.

Youth Wellbeing: Nutrition


Dr. Jeanet Ingwersen

Wellbeing is built upon foundations laid in childhood. Research shows a low glycaemic index breakfast can preferentially prevent children’s cognitive performance from declining throughout the morning.

Dr. Ingwersen’s work explores the fundamental link between nutrition and cognition. This seemingly simple finding has profound implications, showing that basic physiological support is directly tied to a child’s ability to learn and engage, highlighting a tangible, scalable way to improve educational outcomes.

Ingwersen, J. A., et al. (2007). Appetite, 49(1), 240-244.

Youth Wellbeing: School Culture


Dr. Anna Haste

Embedding Mental Health Support Teams in schools helps to “change the culture within schools to incorporate mental health as a topic within the fabric of the school.”

Dr. Haste’s research demonstrates a critical shift from a reactive model, which waits for children to be in crisis, to a proactive, preventative model that normalises conversations about mental health and builds a supportive culture for all students.

Haste, A., et al. (2025). Mental Health and Prevention, 39, 200435.

Lived Experience in Health: The Whole Person


Dr. Stephanie Kilinc

Effective care must address the whole person, not just a condition. Research into conditions like adult-onset epilepsy encourages healthcare practitioners to look beyond medical management and consider the impact on a person’s total wellbeing.

Dr. Kilinc’s work into the lived experience of patients has led to the development of new self-management frameworks. It is a powerful reminder that a condition is something a person has, not who they are, and that effective care must address the whole person.

Kilinc, S. (n.d.). Long-term health conditions. Teesside University.

Lived Experience in Health: Social Context


Dr. Mia Campbell

Support for chronic illness must be designed with a deep understanding of real-world barriers. Self-management is challenging for people in deprivation due to “financial constraints and health literacy.”

Dr. Campbell’s work adds a crucial layer of social context, exploring how socioeconomic factors impact a person’s ability to manage their health. Her systematic review is vital for tackling health inequalities, showing that effective psychological interventions cannot be context-blind.

Campbell, M., et al. (2023). Self-management of multiple long-term conditions…

Navigating Grief and Loss in Childhood


Maggie Jackson

Children’s picture books can “offer a potentially safe way to allow adults and children to talk about and ask questions about death together.” This approach helps demystify a difficult topic and fosters emotional literacy from an early age.

Maggie Jackson’s research in Death Education focuses on addressing societal silence around loss in a gentle way. Her work shows the value of approaching this not with a specific therapeutic goal, but as a normal part of life. By using picture books, children and adults are provided with a shared language and narrative to process one of life’s most profound experiences.

Jackson, M. (2019). Sneaking Death Into the Classroom [Conference paper]. Death, Dying and Disposal 14, University of Bath, Bath, United Kingdom.

Section 6: Psychology in a Changing World

New Frontiers and Pressing Concerns.

The Psychology of the Climate Crisis


Dr. Marcin Owczarek

The climate crisis is a profound psychological issue. Foundational work on how to measure emotional responses like eco-anxiety is essential for understanding its true mental health impact and developing effective, evidence-based interventions.

Dr. Marcin Owczarek’s systematic review of the tools used to measure climate-related emotional responses found a need for “more rigorous methodological standards and theoretical clarity”. By ensuring that concepts like eco-anxiety are measured accurately and consistently, psychology can better identify vulnerable populations and develop appropriate support.

Owczarek, M., et al. (2025). Psychometric assessment of climate-related emotional responses… Current Psychology, 44, 4883–4905.

Section 7: Critical Perspectives & Foundational Processes

A Critical Lens on Psychology


Dr. Richard House

Psychology contributes by turning a lens on itself. This work offers a critical perspective on the “therapeutic ethos” in modern culture, encouraging reflection on the cultural and political contexts of the discipline.

Dr. Richard House’s work is vital for the health of the discipline. Publications like Therapy Beyond Modernity challenge foundational assumptions and encourage practitioners and researchers to question professional ideologies that can sometimes limit, rather than expand, genuine understanding and healing.

House, R. (n.d.) Therapy Beyond Modernity.

Investigating Core Cognitive Mechanisms


Dr. Srdan Medimorec

Investigating fundamental cognitive processes like metacognition (thinking about one’s own mind) and implicit learning (learning without awareness) provides the building blocks for understanding all complex human behaviours.

Dr. Srdan Medimorec’s foundational research into the architecture of the mind is crucial, as it helps us understand everything from how we learn new skills to why we are vulnerable to certain biases. This work ensures the discipline remains grounded in a robust understanding of the mind.

Medimorec, S., et al. (2024). Children’s metacognition and cognitive offloading…

Section 8: The Social Work Lens

Practice, Policy, and Inclusivity.

Championing LGBTQ+ Inclusive Practice


Dr. Claire Brown

Research focused on “support and empowerment for LGBTQ+ carers and care-experienced young people” can be used as a tool for advocacy to create more equitable and just social care systems by challenging cisgenderism and heteronormativity.

Dr. Brown’s research provides a conceptually informed analysis of current policy and practice. Through publications such as “Removing gender barriers: Promoting inclusion for trans and non-binary carers in fostering and adoption,” she argues for more consistent inclusion and challenges the ways in which trans people’s voices have been silenced in research.

Brown, C. (2023). Removing gender barriers: Promoting inclusion for trans and non-binary carers in fostering and adoption. Practice, 35(1), 47-62.

Advancing Trauma-Informed Practice


Dr. Rachel Close

Dr. Close’s research explores “the shift towards trauma-informed practice in domestic abuse service provision,” moving beyond acknowledging trauma to systematically investigating how services can adapt their entire approach to be responsive to its impact.

This critical focus examines how a trauma-informed approach affects survivors, practitioners, and organisations. The goal is to provide an evidence base for creating services that are safer, more empowering, and more effective for those who have experienced domestic abuse.

Close, R. (2025). Exploring the shift towards trauma-informed practice… Teesside University.

The Indispensable Contribution

  • The research collectively demonstrates that beneath political and social disruption lie fundamental human processes.
  • By focusing on these mechanisms, psychological science provides tangible pathways toward constructive change.
  • From deconstructing prejudice to amplifying narratives of resilience, the work highlights the discipline’s enduring value in addressing contemporary challenges.
  • In a complex world, the rigorous, evidence-based, and deeply human insights of psychological research are more essential than ever.
