Gambling and sport: What drives behaviour, and what can we do about it?

Stakeholder Event – June 2026

Why this matters

Gambling is becoming part of sport

  • Gambling is now highly visible through advertising, sponsorship, and media coverage
  • It is embedded in match-day routines, fan discussions, and online spaces
  • Many people, particularly younger audiences, encounter gambling as part of everyday sport rather than as a separate activity

We know what is happening – but not fully why

Most research has focused on describing patterns such as how often people gamble or how exposure relates to behaviour.

What is less understood is how social environments – particularly sports groups and fan communities – shape gambling behaviour in more complex ways.

What we wanted to understand

Our key question

How does being part of a sports group shape gambling behaviour, and why does this differ between people?

Three things we focused on

  • Sports engagement: how involved people are in sport (watching, playing, following teams)
  • Social identity: the extent to which people feel a sense of belonging to sports groups
  • Group norms and expectations: what feels typical or expected within those groups

How we approached this

Shaped by lived experience

  • Experts by experience were involved at every stage of the project
  • They helped refine the research questions, improve the language used, and ensure the work was grounded in real-world experience
  • This strengthened both the relevance and the credibility of the findings

Two parts to the study

Survey (Work Package 1)
- Focused on sports-engaged individuals across the UK
- Final validated sample of 266 participants
- Measured gambling behaviour, attitudes, sports engagement, and social identity

Interviews (Work Package 2)
- In-depth conversations with 11 participants
- Explored how gambling is experienced and understood within sports social groups

Going beyond simple description

Rather than only identifying who gambles, we examined how different factors work together.

This allowed us to move beyond simple correlations and understand when and why sport is linked to gambling behaviour.

What we found

Being involved in sport increases gambling

Across the data, higher levels of sports engagement were consistently associated with:

  • More positive attitudes towards gambling
  • Higher expectations of gambling outcomes
  • Increased gambling behaviour and risk

This provides a clear starting point: sport is an important context for understanding gambling.

But not everyone is affected in the same way

Although sport is linked to gambling overall, the strength of this relationship varies significantly between individuals.

Why relationships are not the same for everyone

The relationship between sports engagement and gambling depends on social identity.

This is what we refer to as a moderation effect.

What do we mean by this?

A moderation effect means that the impact of one factor (sport) changes depending on another factor (social identity).

In other words, sport does not have the same effect on everyone.

Think of it like this

  • For some people, greater involvement in sport leads to a strong increase in gambling
  • For others, the same level of involvement has little or no effect

This shows that exposure alone cannot explain behaviour.

Social identity changes the story

Different aspects of identity mattered in different ways:

  • Strong identification with friends was linked to less risky thinking and lower problem gambling
  • Strong identification with broader fan or team norms was linked to more positive attitudes towards gambling and higher risk

A key insight

For individuals with very high levels of social identification, the link between sport and gambling can effectively disappear.

This means that for these individuals, gambling behaviour is no longer driven by how engaged they are in sport, but by the norms and expectations of the group.

What people told us

Gambling is part of everyday sport

Participants consistently described gambling as something encountered through:

  • Match-day routines
  • Conversations with friends and other fans
  • Media, advertising, and online environments

Gambling was not seen as separate from sport, but embedded within it.

It becomes part of belonging

Gambling was often tied to:

  • Shared rituals and traditions
  • Group bonding and humour
  • Expressions of identity as a fan

This made gambling feel like a normal part of being part of the group.

How people move into gambling

The shift from exposure to behaviour was influenced by:

  • Curiosity and emotional investment in sport
  • Peer influence and social pressure
  • A desire to fit in or participate fully in the group

For some, this led to habitual behaviour; for others, it led to resistance or attempts to step back.

People recognise the risks

Participants were not unaware of harms. Many highlighted:

  • Risks for younger people
  • Concerns about the level of exposure
  • The need for stronger regulation, education, and safeguards

What this means

One-size-fits-all approaches won’t work

Treating all sports fans as a single group misses important differences in how and why gambling occurs.

Focus on social environments

Interventions need to engage with the social contexts where gambling is normalised:

  • Friendship groups
  • Sports teams and clubs
  • Fan communities and online spaces

Opportunities for prevention

The findings highlight opportunities to:

  • Strengthen positive peer norms within friendship groups
  • Challenge harmful expectations linked to fan identity
  • Reduce exposure and protect younger audiences

What we do next

Co-producing solutions with stakeholders

  • Use these findings to develop practical recommendations
  • Work collaboratively across public health, sport, and policy sectors
  • Ensure that responses are grounded in both evidence and lived experience
