What’s the point of research at a time like this?

What psychology and social work research at Teesside University can do about the big challenges of our time

Author

Dr Christopher Wilson

Hello everyone. For those of you who don’t know me, my name is Chris Wilson, and I’m an Associate Professor of Psychology here at Teesside University.

It’s my pleasure to welcome you to the first in our new series of research seminars, showcasing some of the exciting work being done by our colleagues in Psychology and Social Work.

At the start of a seminar series, it is traditional to deliver an opening talk, to set the scene for the series.

The way I’ve chosen to do that is to frame this talk around a question that I think many of us might have asked ourselves at some point over the last few years. That question is:

What’s the point of research at a time like this?

We’ve all heard the curse “may you live in interesting times.”

I think it’s fair to say that we are certainly living in interesting times.

The challenges we face

  • Climate change
  • Misinformation and distrust
  • Social Isolation
  • Inequality and division
  • Trauma and mental health issues
  • Chronic illness and ageing populations
  • Adaptation to a rapidly changing world

There are many challenges that we face, on a global, community, and individual level.

Challenges like climate change are co-occurring with increasing distrust of institutions and misinformation, increasing social isolation, rising inequality…

and a general sense of instability and uncertainty in a rapidly changing world.

We know that people can often feel that these challenges are overwhelming, and that they are too big for any one person to tackle.

This can be true for academics and researchers as well. When research is framed as a luxury, something that we do when we have the time and resources, it can be framed as a distraction from the “real work” of addressing these challenges.

The Human Element

  • Contemporary challenges like misinformation, social isolation, and trauma are often viewed through a political or economic lens.
  • At their core, however, they are fundamentally human problems, rooted in cognition, emotion, and social identity.
  • Research provides us with the tools to understand these challenges, to support individuals and communities, and to start looking for solutions.

The goal of this talk is to argue that research is not a luxury, but a necessity. The problems we face are fundamentally human problems, rooted in cognition, emotion, and social identity.

That we need research in psychology and social work to understand these challenges, to support individuals and communities, and to start looking for solutions.

And importantly, that the research being done here at Teesside University is directly relevant to these tackling these challenges … and making a positive difference in the world.

So in this talk, I am going to highlight some of the research being done by our colleagues in Psychology and Social Work here at Teesside University.

I have selected a few themes based on what I have just outlined, and I will highlight some of the research being done in each area.

This is not an exhaustive list, and I apologise to anyone whose work I have not been able to include.

But I hope that this will give you a sense of the amazing work being done here, and whet your appetite for the seminars to come.

Navigating the information maze

How our emotions are affecting what we believe and the decision we make.

Perhaps one of the biggest challenges of our time is the amount of information available to us, shaping beliefs and behaviours in ways that can be either helpful or harmful.

The WHO have used the phrase “infodemic” to describe an outbreak of misinformation that can have serious public health consequences.

There is a body of work here in our department that is helping us understand these processes.

Understanding how people process information, how emotions shape our judgments, and how social identity influences our sense of truth is essential for developing effective strategies to counter misinformation and promote healthier public discourse.

The Psychology of the Climate Crisis

Dr. Marcin Owczarek

Dr. Owczarek’s work on how to measure emotional responses like eco-anxiety is essential for understanding the psychological impact.

Let’s start with one of the biggest challenges.

There’s a lot of focus on this topic about action, and rightly so. But if we want people to act, we need to understand more about the psychological barriers to action.

Marcin’s work focuses on the concept of eco-anxiety and is leading the call for “more rigorous methodological standards and theoretical clarity”.

This will help advance our understanding of how people are emotionally responding to the climate crisis, and how we can support them in taking action.

Owczarek, M., et al. (2025). Psychometric assessment of climate-related emotional responses… Current Psychology, 44, 4883–4905.

The Paradox of Fear

Dr. Lee Copping

For individuals with strong conspiratorial beliefs, fear-based health appeals can be counterproductive, triggering defiance instead of compliance.

“Instead, the more anxious they become about infection, the more they blame the system and react against it…”

That links to Lee’s work, which demonstrates that how we communicate about risk might not effect behaviour in the way we expect.

It demonstrates the psychological principle of reactance, where a perceived attempt at control triggers defiance.

For individuals who already distrust institutions, fear appeals are not seen as warnings but as coercion, leading to an active rejection of recommended behaviours.

This work is valuable beyond the context of health messaging, because it provides insights into how to communicate effectively in a polarized information environment.

Copping, L. (2022). Anxiety and covid-19 compliance behaviors in the UK: The moderating role of conspiratorial thinking. Personality and Individual Differences, 192, 111604.

Emotion shortcuts our judgement

Dr. Chris Wilson

This research investigates how mental strain (cognitive load) and our personal framing of a choice (construal) can disrupt the ability to process risk, identifying the brain activity behind these altered decisions.

This work shows that under cognitive load (mental stress), certain factors interact, disrupting our ability to rationally assess risk.

It’s crucial for understanding why poor decisions might be made under pressure, and it identifies the corresponding neurological and behavioural patterns.

Wilson, C. J. (2025). Neurological and Behavioural Correlates of Construal in Economic Decision-Making Under Cognitive Load. Behavioural Brain Research, 115829.

Co-Constructing Health Knowledge

Dr. Lauren Walker

The wealth of online information is creating internet-informed patients. To maintain trust in the relationship, doctors need to shift their communication strategy “from compliance to co-construction” to build a shared understanding.

Of course, in times of difficulty, people seek out information to help them make sense things. Like when faced with a health crisis.

Dr. Walker’s research shows that the potential harms of online misinformation can be mitigated through a strong, trusting therapeutic relationship.

When patients feel safe to disclose their online research, it allows clinicians to address misconceptions, validate correct information, and integrate the patient’s priorities into a shared decision-making process.

Walker, L., & Sillence, E. (2024). Examining Healthcare Professionals’ Communication Around Decision-Making with Internet-Informed Patients. Health communication, 39(6), 1094–1101.

How we engage on cognitive offloading

Dr. Srdan Medimorec

Investigating fundamental cognitive processes like metacognition (thinking about one’s own mind) and implicit learning (learning without awareness) provides the building blocks for understanding all complex human behaviours.

Dr. Srdan Medimorec’s into Cognitive offloading - the idea that when we are trying to process large amounts of information use certain techniques to temporarily offload that information so we can handle it

provides really important insights into how we manage this information overload without even realizing it.

This is one of those fundamental processes that underpins so many of the complex behaviours we are talking about today.

Medimorec, S., et al. (2024). Children’s metacognition and cognitive offloading…

The Fraying of Social Bonds

Understanding and addressing loneliness in increasingly factional world.

A report from the WHO Commission on Social Connection found that social isolation and loneliness are widespread, with serious but under-recognized impacts on health, well-being, and society.

Calls on policy-makers, researchers and all sectors to treat social health with the same urgency as physical and mental health.

There is a fascinating cluster of work in the department which has addressed the concepts of not just isolation, but social cohesion and social identity and how it can affect different groups of people.

The Human-Animal Bond

Dr. Heather Clements

Dr. Clements’ research reveals a complex reality about pets and loneliness. With unexpected results around pet ownership and mental health, and evidence of different impacts based on the type of animal and level of engagement.

One of the things that I like about this pandemic-based research is that it provided an unprecedented opportunity to study the human-animal bond in a context where social isolation was widespread.

The findings challenge some common assumptions, but this type of nuanced understanding is vital for developing effective interventions that leverage the human-animal bond to enhance wellbeing.

Clements, H., et al. (2021). Companion Animal Type and Level of Engagement Matter: A Mixed-Methods Study… Animals, 11(8), 2349.

Therapeutic Responses to Marginalisation

Dr. Alex Kyriakopoulos

For destitute asylum seekers, loneliness is an active product of social exclusion. The research highlights that a therapist’s validating presence can be a lifeline, directly counteracting the dehumanising effects of their circumstances.

This work powerfully illustrates that loneliness is not merely a passive state. For marginalised groups, it is often an active product of social and political forces.

The psychological response, therefore, must be equally active. It is not about teaching “social skills” but about the fundamental human act of creating a safe, trusting, and affirming relationship, providing an antidote to the isolation engendered by systemic hostility.

Gerayeli, E., & Kyriakopoulos, A. (2024). How do therapists in the UK navigate the issue of loneliness when working with destitute asylum seekers?… Counselling and Psychotherapy Research.

The Power of Group Identity: Belonging

Dr. Paul Sander

A student’s belief that “I can do this” is powerfully reinforced by the feeling that “I belong here, with these people.” Social identification is a key predictor of wellbeing and academic confidence.

Paul’s research on “Academic Behavioural Confidence” shows that a student’s confidence is not formed in a vacuum but is powerfully reinforced by social identification and a feeling of belonging. This work shows that fostering community is a powerful, evidence-based strategy for promoting individual success.

de la Fuente, J., Sander, P… (2021). Self-Regulation and Regulatory Teaching as Determinants of Academic Behavioral Confidence and Procrastination in Undergraduate Students. Frontiers in Psychology.

The Power of Group Identity: Application

Dr. Robert Portman

The practical application of the social identity approach can be used to facilitate health-enhancing behaviour change by actively building supportive and cohesive communities.

Dr. Portman’s work shows how social identity can influence not just how we feel, but our behaviour. His research demonstrates that when people feel part of a community pursuing shared goals, their confidence and success rates improve.

Portman, R. (2021). An exploration and intervention development…

How we see the faces of others

Dr. Natalie Butcher

Understanding the cognitive neuroscience of face recognition, such as how facial motion affects recognition of same and other race faces, has profound implications for eyewitness testimony, unconscious bias, and intergroup relations.

Dr. Natalie Butcher’s research on the cognitive neuroscience of face recognition could have fit into several of the themes I’m discussing today, because it has such broad implications.

How we recognise and categorise the faces of other people is such a fundamental social cognitive process, with implications for everything from intergroup relations to eyewitness testimony.

Butcher, N. (2009). The effect of facial motion information on the recognition of same and other race faces.

Deconstructing Division

The Psychological Roots of Prejudice and Inequality.

The UN sustainable goals recognise that reducing inequality within and among countries is essential for creating a just and equitable world.

But we know that inequality is rising, both within and between countries. This includes economic inequality, but also social inequality, health inequality, and inequality of opportunity.

Unfortunately, perceptions of inequality can also lead to increased prejudice and division, and the promotion of zero-sum thinking, where one group’s progression is seen to be at the expense of another.

In the department, there is a strong body of work that is addressing these issues, from understanding the linguistic strategies used to disguise prejudice, to developing more equitable health interventions, to promoting inclusive practice in social care.

The Architecture of “Reasonable” Hate

Dr. Shani Burke

A key finding from discursive psychology is that strategies used to disguise talk as ‘not racist’ are also used to justify explicitly racist talk. Understanding these linguistic tactics is the first step toward deconstructing them.

Dr. Burke’s research moves beyond simply labelling discourse as hateful and instead performs a fine-grained analysis of the rhetorical strategies used to make such views appear legitimate.

By analysing tactics like appealing to “common sense” or constructing minorities as a threat, her research provides a “playbook” for how prejudice is laundered into seemingly acceptable public discourse. This work is vital for fostering a more critical and resilient civic dialogue.

Burke, S., & Demasi, M. (2021). Extreme prejudice as reasoned discourse in face-to-face Interactions. In Political Communication: Discursive Perspectives.

Co-Production and Health Equity

Professor Judith Eberhardt

“Through co-producing this intervention with Black communities and delivering it in trusted spaces, we aim to break down barriers to screening and facilitate equitable access to early diagnosis and treatment”.

Prof. Eberhardt’s research exemplifies a critical evolution in tackling systemic inequality.

The concept of “co-production” rejects a model where outside “experts” design interventions for a community. Instead, it involves working with community members as equal partners. This process builds trust and ensures the final product is culturally resonant, providing a powerful, evidence-based blueprint for tackling health inequalities.

Christie-de Jong, F., & Eberhardt, J. (2025). EQUITy in Black Adult health (EQUITA). National Institute for Health and Care Research.

Championing LGBTQ+ Inclusive Practice

Dr. Claire Brown

Research focused on “support and empowerment for LGBTQ+ carers and care-experienced young people” can be used as a tool for advocacy to create more equitable and just social care systems by challenging cisgenderism and heteronormativity.

Dr. Brown’s research provides a conceptually informed analysis of current policy and practice. Through publications such as “Removing gender barriers: Promoting inclusion for trans and non-binary carers in fostering and adoption,” she argues for more consistent inclusion and challenges the ways in which trans people’s voices have been silenced in research.

Brown, C. (2023). Removing gender barriers: Promoting inclusion for trans and non-binary carers in fostering and adoption. Practice, 35(1), 47-62.

Stigma and Reintegration

Dr. Laura Gair

Public stigma against former offenders, “fueled by media portrayals and misconceptions,” can create a self-defeating feedback loop. It creates barriers to the very factors known to reduce reoffending, like stable housing and employment.

Dr. Gair’s research highlights a dangerous societal dynamic. Public stigma creates political pressure for policies that prioritise punishment over evidence-based rehabilitation. These policies, in turn, make successful reintegration nearly impossible, potentially undermining the goal of enhancing long-term public safety. Her work demonstrates the vital role of forensic psychology in bringing evidence to a highly emotional public debate.

Tuschick, E., et al. (2025). A systematic review of public views on the reintegration of men convicted of a sexual offense into the community. Trauma, Violence, & Abuse.

Inequality Within Systems: Recruitment

Dr. Sarah Craven-Staines

Inequality can be embedded within the very systems designed to provide care. This research focuses on creating anti-racist recruitment processes in Clinical Psychology to tackle disparity at its source.

Dr. Craven-Staines’ work is a powerful example of how psychology can apply its principles to its own gatekeeping functions. By systematically examining and aiming to reform the processes by which individuals enter the profession, this research works to ensure that the future of the profession more accurately reflects the diversity of the society it serves.

Craven-Staines, S., et al. (2023). Clinical Psychology Forum, 371, 45-50.

From Trauma to Resilience

Pathways to Healing and Growth.

Trauma is a pervasive issue, with some research suggesting that up to 70% of people will experience at least one traumatic event in their lifetime.

An estimated 3.9% of the global population has experienced PTSD, according to the WHO. We are increasingly recognising that trauma is not just an individual issue, but a societal one, with implications for public health, social policy, and community wellbeing.

There is a strong body of work in the department that is addressing trauma from multiple angles, from understanding how individuals can thrive despite adversity, to exploring the narratives of post-traumatic growth, to examining the long-term impacts of trauma across the lifespan.

Trauma in Higher Education

Dr. Nikki Carthy

This work identified a “flourishing profile” of students who, despite high levels of trauma exposure, had a positive academic experience. This provides a roadmap for universities to proactively foster protective factors like social support and coping skills.

This research represents a crucial move away from a deterministic model where trauma inevitably leads to negative outcomes.

by demonstrating that a subset of students can flourish despite adversity, The implication is that institutions can play an active role in creating an environment where students who have experienced trauma are given the tools to thrive.

Allen, S., et al. (2024). A latent profile analysis of psychosocial factors and trauma exposure in U.K. students… Psychological Trauma: Theory, Research, Practice, and Policy.

Narratives of Post-Traumatic Growth

Dr. Cat O’ Neill

The “Stories of Hope” project uses digital storytelling with men who have experienced post-traumatic growth after a suicide attempt. This research focuses not on risk factors but on the life that comes after, exploring positive psychological changes.

This project exemplifies a vital shift in focus within trauma studies—from pathology to resilience and meaning-making. The methodology of digital storytelling is significant, positioning the men as active creators and experts in their own lived experience. By amplifying stories of hope, this research challenges narratives of despair and provides tangible examples of how it is possible to find new meaning and purpose in the wake of profound suffering.

O’Neill, C., et al. (2025). Stories of Hope: Using Digital Storytelling with men who have experienced post-traumatic growth following a suicide attempt.

The Long Legacy of Trauma

Dr. Kathy Burrows

Trauma has a long legacy. This research focuses on the need for specialised, informed mental health services for adult survivors of childhood sexual abuse, addressing wounds carried across the lifespan.

Dr. Kathy Burrows’ research highlights the long-term consequences of early life adversity and the critical need for informed mental health services that can address these complex issues. By focusing on the professional perspective, this research also illuminates the systemic requirements for effectively supporting survivors.

Burrows, K. (2001). Clinical Psychology Forum, 2001(2), 25-29.

Advancing Trauma-Informed Practice

Dr. Rachel Close

Dr. Close’s research explores “the shift towards trauma-informed practice in domestic abuse service provision,” moving beyond acknowledging trauma to systematically investigating how services can adapt their entire approach to be responsive to its impact.

This critical focus examines how a trauma-informed approach affects survivors, practitioners, and organisations. The goal is to provide an evidence base for creating services that are safer, more empowering, and more effective for those who have experienced domestic abuse.

Close, R. (2025). Exploring the shift towards trauma-informed practice… Teesside University.

The Complexity of Trauma

Dr. Alan Bowman

The deepest wounds of trauma are not just about re-experiencing the past, but about how it shatters a person’s sense of self, creating “Identity loss and existential fear.”

Dr Bowman notes the lack of empirical evidence for interventions tailored specifically for individuals with traumatic injuries.

Dr. Bowman’s clinical work broadens our understanding of trauma’s reach. His focus recognizes that healing pushes the psychological response beyond symptom management to the more profound work of helping individuals reconstruct meaning, purpose, and a coherent sense of identity in the aftermath of life-altering experiences.

Grant, E., Bowman, A., & Johnson, L. (2025). Effectiveness of interventions for psychological distress following traumatic injury: A systematic review. Injury, 56(2), 112090.

Fostering Wellbeing Across the Lifespan

Childhood development, lifelong health, and ageing well.

Wellbeing is a complex, multifaceted concept that encompasses physical, mental, and social health. It is influenced by a wide range of factors, including genetics, environment, lifestyle, and social connections.

In a world where chronic diseases are on the rise, and mental health challenges are increasingly recognised, understanding how to foster wellbeing across the lifespan is more important than ever.

There is a strong body of work in the department that is addressing wellbeing from multiple angles, from understanding the role of nutrition in cognitive development, to exploring the impact of school culture on mental health, to examining the lived experience of chronic illness.

Youth Wellbeing: Nutrition

Dr. Jeanet Ingwersen

Wellbeing is built upon foundations laid in childhood. Research shows a low glycaemic index breakfast can preferentially prevent children’s cognitive performance from declining throughout the morning.

Dr. Ingwersen’s work explores the fundamental link between nutrition and cognition. This seemingly simple finding has profound implications, showing that basic physiological support is directly tied to a child’s ability to learn and engage, highlighting a tangible, scalable way to improve educational outcomes.

Ingwersen, J. A., et al. (2007). Appetite, 49(1), 240-244.

Youth Wellbeing: School Culture

Dr. Anna Haste

Embedding Mental Health Support Teams in schools helps to “change the culture within schools to incorporate mental health as a topic within the fabric of the school.”

Dr. Haste’s research demonstrates a critical shift from a reactive model, which waits for children to be in crisis, to a proactive, preventative model that normalises conversations about mental health and builds a supportive culture for all students.

Haste, A., et al. (2025). Mental Health and Prevention, 39, 200435.

Lived Experience in Health: The Whole Person

Dr. Stephanie Kilinc

Effective care must address the whole person, not just a condition. Research into conditions like adult-onset epilepsy encourages healthcare practitioners to look beyond medical management and consider the impact on a person’s total wellbeing.

Dr. Kilinc’s work into the lived experience of patients has led to the development of new self-management frameworks. It is a powerful reminder that a condition is something a person has, not who they are, and that effective care must address the whole person.

Kilinc, S. (n.d.). Long-term health conditions. Teesside University.

Coping with chronic illness

Dr. Grant McGeechan

Dr McGeechan’s research in psycho-oncology focuses on understanding “how people shape their attitudes towards cancer screening, and how they cope with cancer as a chronic illness”.

This work is essential for developing interventions that support patients not just medically, but also psychologically, through their cancer journey.

McGeechan, G. J., Byrnes, K., Campbell, M., Carthy, N., Eberhardt, J., Paton, W., … & Giles, E. L. (2022). A systematic review and qualitative synthesis of the experience of living with colorectal cancer as a chronic illness. Psychology & health, 37(3), 350-374.

Lived Experience in Health: Social Context

Dr. Mia Campbell

Support for chronic illness must be designed with a deep understanding of real-world barriers. Self-management is challenging for people in deprivation due to “financial constraints and health literacy.”

Dr. Campbell’s work adds a crucial layer of social context, exploring how socioeconomic factors impact a person’s ability to manage their health. Her systematic review is vital for tackling health inequalities, showing that effective psychological interventions cannot be context-blind.

Campbell, M., et al. (2023). Self-management of multiple long-term conditions…

Navigating Grief and Loss in Childhood

Maggie Jackson

Children’s picture books can “offer a potentially safe way to allow adults and children to talk about and ask questions about death together.” This approach helps demystify a difficult topic and fosters emotional literacy from an early age.

Maggie Jackson’s research in Death Education focuses on addressing societal silence around loss in a gentle way. Her work shows the value of approaching this not with a specific therapeutic goal, but as a normal part of life. By using picture books, children and adults are provided with a shared language and narrative to process one of life’s most profound experiences.

Jackson, M. (2019). Sneaking Death Into the Classroom [Conference paper]. Death, Dying and Disposal 14, University of Bath, Bath, United Kingdom.

The Value of Psychology and Social Work Research

  • Beneath environmental, political and social disruption lie fundamental human processes.
  • In a complex world, the evidence-based, but deeply human insights of this research are more essential than ever.
  • The work done at Teesside University focuses on the mechanisms that underlie these challenges and can be harnessed to create meaningful change.

To conclude, I hope this brief overview has given you a sense of the breadth and depth of research being conducted in the Department of Psychology and Social Work at Teesside University.

We can see that the work we do here is vital to understanding the complex human behaviour that underpins many of the biggest challenges we face today.

I hope that this has whet your appetite to learn more about our research and to attend the seminar series in the coming months where the researchers will be presenting new work in much more detail.
